# Crafting System

## Design Goal

Crafting should support preparation, not busywork. It should make the player think about the case ahead, not drown them in trash collection and menu friction.

The question behind crafting should be:
What do I need for this situation?

Not:
How many random plants can I hoard?

## Core Principle

Crafting is part of the Warden's field preparation loop.

The player studies a threat, predicts the kind of pressure they are walking into, and prepares tools that matter. Crafting exists to create readiness, edge, and identity.

## System Buckets

### 1. Field Consumables

Short-use preparations for immediate survival and tactical advantage.

Examples:

- anti-rot poultices
- fear-dampening draughts
- bleed stanching wraps
- marsh-fire oil
- bitter salts against tainted water
- smoke charges for confusion or cover

Purpose:
Help the player adapt to environmental and enemy-specific pressure.

### 2. Warden Tools

Professional tools that support investigation, control, and field authority.

Examples:

- tracking markers
- trap kits
- evidence seals
- burial chalk
- ward stakes
- dissection rolls
- lock tags and chain pins

Purpose:
Extend the player's capability to read scenes, control routes, secure bodies, and shape outcomes before combat begins.

### 3. Special Preparations

Rare, expensive, or case-specific tools for major threats and key quest moments.

Examples:

- funeral seals for disturbed dead
- flood-ward compounds for fen zones
- bait compounds for specific creatures
- resin binders for containment
- sanctioned tinctures tied to doctrine use

Purpose:
Make major cases feel serious and reward forethought without turning every encounter into alchemy theater.

## Resource Philosophy

Resources should be limited, legible, and thematically grounded.

Rules:

- keep the resource set compact
- avoid dozens of nearly identical herbs and scraps
- favor memorable materials over generic clutter
- let some ingredients come from trade, favors, salvage, and monster remains, not only harvesting

The player should recognize what matters without needing a spreadsheet brain.

## Relationship to Investigation

Crafting should depend on knowledge.

Examples:

- learning that an enemy uses fear changes what the player prepares
- discovering contaminated water changes route and supply choices
- identifying ritual residue unlocks a Lantern-style preparation path

The better the investigation, the more precise the preparation.

## Relationship to Doctrine Paths

Each doctrine should value crafting differently:

- Judicator uses seals, restraints, and procedural control tools
- Stalker relies on traps, field kits, and terrain-responsive consumables
- Lantern uses wards, rites, protections, and residue-specific preparations
- Broker uses improvised leverage tools, bribe goods, poisons, and asymmetrical advantages

Shared crafting exists for all players, but doctrine emphasis changes what feels essential.

## UI and Tempo Rule

Crafting should be fast and readable.

Preferred behavior:

- craft from camp, ward-house, workshop, or travel rest point
- keep recipe logic understandable at a glance
- let quest preparation highlight relevant options
- avoid forcing constant inventory micromanagement mid-mission

The player should feel sharper after preparation, not interrupted by it.

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- junk-heavy harvesting loops
- dozens of low-value recipes
- recipe trees that exist only to pad progression
- crafting systems that outperform actual tactical play
- material names so abstract the player cannot infer purpose

## Current Design Decision

Crafting will be a case-facing preparation system built around:

- Field Consumables
- Warden Tools
- Special Preparations

This system is now part of the core game identity.
